Equations & Derivation

Step 1 — Transit depth

δ = (Rp / R★)²
Fraction of stellar flux blocked during transit. For Earth–Sun: δ ≈ (R⊕/R☉)² ≈ 84 ppm.

Step 2 — SNR after N transits

SNR = δ·√N / σ
Noise averages down as 1/√N. Detection requires SNR ≥ SNRmin (typically 7–10).
Nmin = (SNRmin · σ / δ)²
Minimum number of transits needed to reach the detection threshold.

Step 3 — Convert transits to time

Tmin = Nmin · P
Where P is the orbital period. Substituting Nmin:
Tmin = (SNRmin · σ)² · P / δ²
The cleanest combined form before substituting for δ and P.

Step 4 — Kepler's third law

P = a3/2 / √M★
Period in years when a is in AU and M★ is in solar masses.

Final — Main detection equation

Tmin = (SNRmin · σ)² · (R★/Rp)⁴ · a3/2 / √M★
The full equation. Shows that detection difficulty scales as Rp−4 (extremely sensitive to planet size) and a3/2 (orbital distance via period).

Step 5 — Transit probability

Ptransit = R★ / a
Geometric probability for a circular orbit. Earth–Sun: ≈ 0.0047 (~1 in 215).

Search burden (incl. geometry)

Tsearch = Tmin / Ptransit
Effective time to find a transiting Earth analog among random Sun-like stars. Not observing time — survey burden.
Practical refinement: Most surveys require at least 2–3 observed transits for validation, so Tmin = max(Nmin,SNR · P , Nmin,transit · P). This calculator uses whichever condition is more constraining.

Detection difficulty scaling

2× smaller planet → 16× longer Tmin
2× farther orbit → 2.8× longer Tmin
2× larger star → 16× longer Tmin
2× better noise → 4× shorter Tmin
M-dwarf host → 10–100× easier
